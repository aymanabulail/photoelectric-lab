Pre-Lab Theory & Conceptual Questions
Complete all pre-lab questions before proceeding. These must be submitted to your TA before you begin the experiment. Show all derivations and reasoning.
Question 1 — Planck's Quantum Hypothesis

Einstein explained the photoelectric effect by proposing that light consists of discrete quanta (photons). Using Planck's relation E = hf, derive an expression for the maximum kinetic energy Kmax of emitted photoelectrons in terms of the photon frequency f, Planck's constant h, and the work function φ of the metal. Explain the physical meaning of each term.

Question 2 — Threshold Frequency & Stopping Potential

Define the threshold (cutoff) frequency f₀. Derive an expression for it. Then explain the concept of stopping potential Vs. Show that eVs = Kmax = hf − φ. What would a graph of Vs vs. frequency f look like, and what physical quantities can be extracted from its slope and y-intercept?

Question 3 — Wave vs. Quantum Predictions

Classical wave theory predicts that: (a) any frequency of light should eventually eject electrons if intensity is high enough, and (b) Kmax should increase with intensity. Quantum theory predicts the opposite for both. State the quantum predictions clearly, and explain how the photoelectric effect provided decisive evidence against the classical wave model. What role did Einstein's 1905 paper play?

Question 4 — Work Function & Material Dependence

The work function φ depends on the cathode material. Given that the work functions for cesium (Cs), sodium (Na), potassium (K), and platinum (Pt) are approximately 2.1 eV, 2.3 eV, 2.3 eV, and 5.65 eV respectively: (a) Calculate the threshold wavelength for each metal. (b) Which metals can be activated by visible light (400–700 nm)? (c) Why does platinum require UV light? Use h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s and c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s.

Question 5 — Millikan's Experiment & Determination of h

Robert Millikan measured Planck's constant via the photoelectric effect. He plotted stopping potential Vs against frequency f for several metals. From the slope of Vs vs. f, he obtained h/e. If his best-fit line had slope m = 4.124 × 10⁻¹⁵ V·s, calculate h (use e = 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C). Compare your result to the accepted value h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s and compute the percent error. Discuss sources of systematic error in his experiment.

Question 6 — Photon Momentum & de Broglie Wavelength

A photon has momentum p = h/λ = hf/c. (a) Compute the momentum of a UV photon with λ = 200 nm. (b) If this photon ejects an electron from sodium (φ = 2.3 eV), what is the maximum speed of the emitted electron? (c) Calculate the de Broglie wavelength of that electron. (d) Is relativistic treatment necessary? Justify using v/c.

  • Determine the work function φ of different metals from the y-intercept of the Vs vs. f graph.
  • Investigate how light intensity affects the photocurrent but not the stopping potential.

Task 1 — Familiarization (5 min)
Open the simulation tab. Set the metal to sodium. Set intensity to 50%. Slowly sweep the frequency slider from 4 × 10¹⁴ Hz to 12 × 10¹⁴ Hz. Observe when emission begins, the color of the light, and how Kmax changes. Record qualitative observations in Table 1.
Task 2 — Threshold frequency measurement (10 min)
For each metal (Na, K, Cs, Zn, Pt), find the minimum frequency at which emission begins. Record this threshold frequency f₀ in Table 2. Calculate the experimental work function φ = hf₀ for each metal (use h = 4.136 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV·s). Compare to accepted values and compute percent error.
Task 3 — Stopping potential vs. frequency (15 min)
Using sodium, vary the frequency from just above threshold to 12 × 10¹⁴ Hz in steps of ~0.5 × 10¹⁴ Hz (at least 8 data points). Record Vs and f in Table 3. In the analysis section, plot Vs vs. f and perform a linear regression. Extract h from the slope and φ from the intercept.
Task 4 — Effect of intensity (10 min)
Set frequency to 8.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz with sodium. Vary intensity from 10% to 100% in steps of 10%. For each intensity, record: (a) the photocurrent and (b) the stopping potential. Enter data in Table 4. Do your results confirm the quantum model?
Task 5 — Multi-metal comparison (10 min)
Set frequency to 9.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz. For each metal, record Vs and Kmax. Plot Kmax vs. metal work function. Explain the trend. Which metal has the most energetic emitted electrons at this frequency? Why?
Task 6 — Planck's constant determination
Using data from Task 3, perform least-squares linear regression on Vs vs. f. The slope equals h/e. Calculate h and compare to accepted value. Report your percent error and discuss systematic uncertainties.

Questions Post-Lab Discussion Questions
Question 1

In your stopping potential vs. frequency graph, the slope is h/e. Why is it h/e and not just h? How does this allow you to determine h if e is known? What would happen to the slope if you repeated the experiment with a different metal? Justify with equations.

Question 2

You observe that doubling the light intensity doubles the photocurrent but does not change the stopping potential. Explain this result using the photon model. What does the photocurrent depend on physically? What does the stopping potential depend on? Would a classical wave physicist have predicted this?

Question 3

In real photoelectric experiments, the photocurrent does not drop to exactly zero at the stopping potential — there is a small "dark current." Propose at least two physical mechanisms that could cause electrons to reach the anode even when V ≥ Vs.

Question 4

The work function values for the same metal can vary significantly depending on its surface preparation, crystal orientation, and contamination. Sodium's work function ranges from about 2.1 to 2.75 eV depending on surface conditions. How would this affect your measurement of the y-intercept in the Vs vs. f graph? How does it affect the slope (and thus your determination of h)?

Question 5

The photoelectric effect is used in photomultiplier tubes (PMTs), solar cells, and digital camera sensors. Choose one application and explain how the photoelectric effect is exploited in its operation. What material properties are optimized, and why?

Question 6 — Extension

Compton scattering (1923) further confirmed the photon model by showing that X-ray photons have momentum p = h/λ. Compare Compton scattering and the photoelectric effect as evidence for photon particle behavior. Why was the photoelectric effect insufficient alone to convince the physics community?
